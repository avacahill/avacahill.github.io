About Me

Comparative psychologist curious about what dogs know.

I'm a first-year PhD student in the Department of Psychological and Brain Sciences at Johns Hopkins University, where I work in Dr. Christopher Krupenye's lab. My research centers on comparative cognition — understanding how different species perceive, learn from, and communicate with others.

My undergraduate work at Boston College focused on how domestication has shaped dogs' responses to communicative cues, comparing them with dingoes — a semi-domesticated relative. This work became my honors thesis and a manuscript currently under review.

Outside the lab, I love the outdoors (I'm a certified wilderness first aider and outdoor trip leader), radio (former DJ and AM coordinator at WZBC 90.3 FM), and graphic design.

Research

Projects & Publications

🐕

Novel Marker Cue in Dogs and Dingoes

How has domestication shaped animals' responses to communicative cues? This project compares dogs (Canis familiaris) and dingoes (Canis dingo) on a novel marker task to understand the evolutionary pressures behind social learning abilities.

Under Review

Cahill, A., Byrne, M., Palermo, S., Johnston, A.M. — Journal submission pending

🧠

Theory of Mind in Dogs

Can dogs infer the content of others' minds? I am currently supervising undergraduate thesis student Fiorella Durand on this question, advising on experimental design, data collection, and analysis. This project was awarded a Provost's Undergraduate Research Award fellowship (Summer 2026).

👶

Children's Social Cognition

As a research assistant at the Boston College Canine Cognition Center, I also collected data from 30+ children across 3 projects, exploring developmental parallels between human and non-human social learning.
